Why is the USA's largest producer of architectural art named after a pineapple grove?

Pineapple Grove Designs was named in honor of a local hero who endured economic and racial hardship yet still became a local pioneer who contributed more than most to our community.

In 1903, Florida's first Japanese settlers found an area of scrub land that would soon be established as the Japanese farming community of Yamato Colony. Yamato translates to "great peace." An important member of the colony was Sukeji Morikami, known as "George." By 1908, the Yamato Colony had planted a 42 acre grove of pineapples. Soon the entire area would become pineapple central, second only to Hawaii. Fast forward to 1974 when George Morikami donated 200 acres of his land to the State of Florida for a Japanese garden park.

In honor of Mr. Morikami's lifetime of hard work and generosity The City of Delray Beach entered into a Sister City agreement with Miyazu, Kyoto, Japan, George's birthplace and created the Pineapple Grove Arts District to encourage art and culture in the City's downtown. The first art studio in the art district was started in a small garage by Sculptor Christopher O'Hare. It was called Pineapple Grove Designs. Today Pineapple Grove Designs has grown to occupy over 6 acres and 80,000 square feet of offices, art studio and production facility. Over 24,000 architects, designers and builders have trusted Pineapple Grove Designs on thousands of projects throughout the USA and around the world. The founders and staff of Pineapple Grove Designs continue to be inspired by the generosity and work ethic of Mr. Morikami.
